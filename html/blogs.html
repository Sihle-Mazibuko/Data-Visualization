Overview of Data Visualization

This reading on data visualizations provides a multitude of insights regarding what exactly data visualisation is, breaking down the components that makeup data visualization (data and information), the various ways in which data visualization is depicted and how we interpret the data being depicted.

Defining data visualization:

Being foreign to the concept of visualization I did not have a clear grasp of the topic. Li and contributors such as Mackinlay, Schneidermann, Kirk, Reyna, Nelson, etc. provided various definitions that provided useful insight on the topic but also allowed me to come up with my definition based on my understanding of the information presented. Data visualization is the process of presenting data graphically, which in turn provides the observer with a way of exploring and analysing data, allowing them to detect patterns, correlations and causes in the data sets provided. From this definition, as well as others in the reading. Data visualization as a process can achieve this by exploring real-time interaction, on-the-fly processing, visual scalability and user assistance and personalization (Li, 2020). It is also important to note that data visualization consists of data which is unprocessed information that cannot be comprehended (which is further split into primary and secondary data), and information is data that is processed and can be understood and read. This definition covers the three main characteristics of data visualization which are the readability of the data, how the data can be recognized from previous iterations and the meaning generated from the form of the visualization. Various visualisation techniques are used to depict data sets, including trees, scatter plots, charts, diagrams, etc. Data visualization can be further understood by classifying it into two categories: information visualization which is more organized and displayed as a way of ensuring users gain the information they may need and scientific visualization whereby the visualization forms are simulated as opposed to those in information visualization which are design control systems. Going back to the forms of data visualization the reading lists numerous amounts but the ones that stood out to me include simulations and volumes and the different types of volumes. This interest is because I have never seen these forms of visualization or the technique interests me. Simulation is the latter as it is more interactive (in some cases) and can display interesting data regarding natural phenomena. Volume is interesting due to the vast forms it can take on thus providing a wide selection of ways to visualize data. Some of these techniques are but are not limited to, emulation which allows transitions to a modern technique from older ones, which uses a low computing complexity (Li, 2020) to visualize certain parts of data and volume rendering which converts 3-dimensional data into a 2-dimensional visualization.

Understanding data visualisation:

Data visualization as practice seems to be a simple and very useful technique which provides various benefits to its users such as being able to take in large amounts of data countering efficiencies, show errors or inaccuracies in data, reveal trends and patterns, tell a story to the viewer, optimizing data reports, cutting labour costs, etc. But these affordances can only be achieved by selecting the correct visualization and for my assessment’s simulations, graphs and tables are seemingly the best techniques to use. Displaying that large amount of data from NASA APIs can be challenging thus using tables and charts (which Li considers to be one the best ways to display data, tables specifically for numerical data) along with some sort of simulation for the user to interact with, the website and its content will be easily readable, understandable by the viewer and information can be easily extracted from it. A practical example of one of the visualisations and its effectiveness is weather.com, which predominately tables that lists weather information regarding any inputted areas. What makes this visualization effective is firstly tables are excellent for numerical values (wind speed, temperature, etc.) as well and the design options afforded by tables allow it to look legible and comprehensible on the website, which can be useful for my design choices.

Critical reflection of proposed UI and UX

My website aims to inform users with various information from NASA's Astronomy Picture of the Day API. To achieve an effective communication of this information specific design principles and user interface elements work together to provide the viewer of the website with an efficient user experience that allows a relatively easy perception and understanding of what they are viewing.

The user interface:

For the user interface, important considerations for this site were colour and layout. Colour is extremely important in all websites as it affects how the user perceives what they see on the website. Keeping this in mind neutral colours such as black and white are found throughout the site. The use of a black background as opposed to traditional white mainly gives the website a more out-of-space feel as space is black but also as having a dark background causes less strain on the eyes when viewing content in darker areas. The combination of a low blue light background (black) and high blue light text (white) ensures the user encounters minimal eye strain when using the site. The moving background, as seen through the white stars, also gives the illusion of travelling through space while traversing through the site. To add to this astronomy theme, there is a wide variety of colours seen on different pages. This is due to space itself having a large variety of colours and the pictures being depicted also following the same trend. It is worth noting that an overload of colours can cause strain on the viewer and as such colours used together be it on the same page or section are either complementary or of the same shade to ensure efficient colour scheming. Having elements placed in the middle and encouraging white space (in this case black) on either side of said element allows the eyes to flow naturally down the page. To further help this, elements are effectively spaced out to discourage clutter and the importance of discouraging clutter is that clutter makes our visuals appear more complicated than necessary (Knaflic, 2015, p.73). This can lead to cognitive load for the viewer as they are unsure of what to view first or what they are even looking at. Going back to the layout, different sections on the site all have their layout techniques to show that specific content belongs to a specific group of information. This was achieved by using a few of the Gestalt Principles of Visual Perception namely: proximity, similarity, and enclosure. Proximity is seen through how each section of the website has spaces around it to show its contents belong to only that specific section. Similarity is seen through how the aforementioned contents of a particular section all have the same traits be it size, colour, or layout. An example of this is in the wireframes section, all the images of the wireframes are the same size, and the captions of each image also have the same colour and text layout. Enclosure is prevalent in each section of the website but each for different reasons. The first example of enclosure is at the bottom of the homepage as the little cards belong to a relevant page and separating them into two individual cards shows each serves its purpose and has its importance. The blog post page also shows enclosure through the menu item being placed in a box once it has been selected. There is minimal iconography used with the only two icons being the burger bar menu icon and the X icon, and this was implemented for smaller screen devices to indicate that they can access the navigation menu by pressing it. The predominant use of text as icons is to avoid users being confused as to what a specific icon means. There are exceptions as some icons are generally known to signify specific ideas however this generalization cannot be used to assume that all users know the same website semantics.

The user experience:

For the user experience, the main focuses were responsiveness, interactivity and establishing an information hierarchy. Firstly, responsiveness is something to always consider as users will not always view the website on one type of medium. Ensuring the content is sufficiently scaled across multiple devices ensures the content is not only limited to one type of screen and the information is always readable. The website can be easily viewed on the most commonly used screen sizes. Interactivity is also essential to any website and in this one, it is seen through the navigation bar. Each text button can lead the user to a specific section of the page and having minimal interactive features and only one navigation menu ensures the user is not overloaded with features, and sometimes they may not know the purpose and function of that feature. Forming an information hierarchy in the site allows the user to know what the purpose of this site is and what they need to know. Upon loading up the site, the user will see a large description followed by a date and an image. This already does two things: the user knows what the site is about and without reading anything else they know the most important information as what you display first tends to be the most important part of the site. As you scroll through the site, different texts have different sizes. This is again to display the importance of certain content. Headings are made bigger as they inform the user of what a particular section is, and paragraph content is placed right under the heading to ensure the user knows that this specific text relates to this overarching topic. Using a sans-serif font throughout the site, namely, Helvetica, ensures the content is readable as sans-serif is considered on the most legible fonts. The site also has a minimalistic feel to it as there are not many elements to it, mostly information. This is firstly because the site aims to inform and only that and also minimalism is considered to be one of the most pleasing aesthetics. Having an aesthetically pleasing site ensures users are more able to use the site.

Understanding why the website exists:

An important consideration when implementing these UI and UX elements of this site was who this website is for. It is expected that if you wish to view this site you have some interest in astronomy and photography. Focusing on the first three reasons, a deduction can be made that these types of people wish to be informed. The conveying of information without overload or having a scarcity of information is difficult to achieve however as seen on the site, users are given enough information relevant to what they need which is a goal that has been met on this website.

What is Interaction for Data Visualization

In this piece of work titled “What is Interaction for Data Visualization" published in 2020 by Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin, the authors delve into the perplexing and confusing nature of the concept of interaction in the context of data visualization. Their primary objective is to establish a clear and precise definition as to what interaction is and how it pertains to data visualization. Dimara and Perin carefully outline the step-by-step process they undertook to formulate a comprehensive definition encompassing the essence of interaction within data visualization. Throughout their exploration, they consider various criteria and factors, ensuring that their definition is all-encompassing and leaves no aspect of interaction in data visualization unaddressed. As a result, this article serves as an exhaustive and illuminating resource for anyone seeking a deep understanding of the intricacies and significance of interaction within the field of data visualization.

Evanthia and Diamara's Research:

It is important to note that a large number of ideas and processes are mentioned in the reading and thus only those relevant to interaction in data visualization and this blog shall be mentioned and discussed. I make this clarity as a result of the research process taken by Dimara and Perin. Although not completely relevant to defining and understanding what interaction is, it is important to understand how these two authors arrived at the conclusion of what exactly is interaction. The authors frequently mention “seeds” (Evanthia & Dimara, 2020) as a term used for the sources involved in the construction of the definition of interaction. When evaluating these sources, a three-part criterion known as the “methodology rationale” (Evanthia & Dimara, 2020) was used when gathering sources. The criteria are, do both the source number and inputs from experts contribute to grasping the viewpoint on interaction, does the source use snowballing (starting with small specific sources and using back and forth referencing thus leading to bias) and does the source include relevant human-computer interaction (HCI) references. The actual collection of the sources involved a four-step process.

The research process:

Firstly, finding 5 papers that provide a definition of interaction. Secondly, expert researchers in the field of interaction suggested papers that define and are linked to interaction. Then the selected papers were scored using impact and relevance scores. Finally, the previous three steps were repeated until no new papers were found. The scoring consists of a complex formula that essentially scored certain tags the papers had from a scale of 0-2 (0 being irrelevant, 1 being sufficient and 2 being well executed). The tags define interaction, how critical they are on interaction, the benefits of interaction, interaction concepts, explaining the interaction pipeline, and to what extent the paper is a formal review. It is important to make note of such a finely detailed and well-thought-out researched method as first it helps get into the mind of the authors to know what they were doing when constructing the definition but also can form a guide for one's research in various future endeavours. Not to say there is one perfect way to research, but the method used by Evanthia and Dimara is impressive in how it thoroughly critiques sources and seeks to only reference and use papers that will aim to aid the defining of interaction.

The results of the research and interaction:

From this process, the authors provide a few frameworks for understanding what interaction is. The first basis introduced is the external and internal entities and actions that allow us to understand what interaction for visualization must involve. The external entities are visual properties (such as the interface of the workspace whereby interaction is set), technological properties (displays and visualisations), humans/users, the actual data, and various modalities (speaking, moving and the setting for the interactivity). The internal entities involve interaction being goal-oriented with goals being data exploration, creating knowledge and creating sense which all are the user's goals for interactivity. External actions are the conversations being had between the viewer which can alter the data being presented in some manner (e.g., filtering out certain content). Internal actions are the understanding and interpretation of the data. To further understand interaction, it is important to know its affordances and limitations. The benefits of interaction include it being a way of increasing the viewer's perception when they are actively dealing with data exploration. Interaction always takes advantage of the viewer's abilities by introducing new types of visualisations, modalities and technological input devices that aid in the interaction process. Interaction is no stranger to its fair share of drawbacks as one of the main critiques is how the goal with interaction has become more so implementing various interaction of visualisations and maximizing them without considerations of accessibility for humans. For example, having a visualization be viewable only as projection looks incredible, however not everyone will be able to access a device or platform that allows them to view this interactive visualisation. This leads to another critique as such barriers can be considered a result of high costs, technological challenges and the lack of theoretical foundations which are all prevalent when constructing interactive data visualizations. Now that interaction and the majority of its complexities are understood a criterion for identifying what is not and how to identify interaction is provided. Not having any perceived reaction indicated a lack of interaction visualization. The user not noticing inputs made by themself notes a lack of interaction. Although a viewer may not fully understand what has happened by simply being aware of a change or action that has occurred that is a sign of visualisation. An interaction can also be perceived as to how it helped the viewer to achieve a desired end goal.

From all of this information a definition of what interaction for data visualization. Interaction in terms of data visualization encompasses the ongoing dialogue between a viewer or user and a specific data interface, categorised by a data-centric purpose, user actions, and the acknowledgement of an interface response as such.

Critical reflection of Data-Driven Artwork

The vision for the site:

Creating artwork can be an impossible task to conquer as the canvas for creativity is large, however, narrowing it down to an artwork that uses data to convey information and also display the artist’s efforts and brilliance should make the task relatively simpler. The requirement of having the artwork relay information makes the task simple as it suggests the artwork must possess some meaning and significance, with emphasis on holding significance. Couple with the currently existing data that includes the most popular words in the previous APOD months, it is then essential to find out what else is popular with regards to space in general as this is a space-themed website. This research regarding the most searched space topics was conducted.

Fetching Data:

According to data gathered from a variety of sources, (Greene, 2019; Mondovo, n.d.; NASA, 2022; Potor, 2020; West Texas A&M University, n.d.) and a spreadsheet made from the data collected the following topics were the most commonly occurring in all of the aforementioned sources.

Findings:

From all of this data collection, a foregone conclusion can be made the universe is common that the universe is the most popular topic. This already makes things harder as the universe is a very large thing with a lot more complex and complicated things within it. As such I turned towards the other most popular terms namely Earth, the Moon, and Stars and this gave me an idea of what to make, some form of star system using the APODs as surface materials for the planets. Another factor in this decision besides these three topics being the most is a TikTok trend whereby users made random objects into planets.

Strenghts and weakness:

The first is completely ignoring the most popular topic in the universe. This does have the potential of drawing away potential users who love looking at things related to the universe; however, this can be countered by how the images can be any APOD and as such a planet at any given time can be the universe. Another major problem, which is consistent in most art is ensuring the users understand what they are interpreting. Providing an explanation does help with this however not everyone wants to or will read a long paragraph or headings detailing what they see, from the first view the user should be able to understand. The strength of this type of data art is that it follows suit to the theme of the website which is displaying beautiful images of our universe. Choosing to have images be planets creates one giant image of a unique star system which can be visually pleasing to view for those who enjoy looking at different planets and star systems.  

Solar system progress

Currently, it is static, and the planets and sun are not APOD pictures however this is a good foundation to work on. The large size of the star system and tiny planets make it synonymous with our solar system with the 4 rocky planets. This ensures some form of familiarity as most people who are aware of the solar system know the four planets and this familiarity ensures viewers already have some form of understanding of what they are viewing. There is the use of the grey container to hold the solar system is for viewing and will not be there in the final product as in the finished product this star system will blend in with the star background making it look like you are looking at a picture of a star system and this can also make the viewer believe this is a unique APOD staying true to the main purpose of this site which is showing beautiful pictures. From here the solar system will be interactable in terms of a hover effect whereby the planets get bigger and information on the planet (such as APOD name, author, and date) will be displayed to ensure viewers understand the star system.

Experience with the data-driven artwork

Designing a solar system using d3.js was a tough but rewarding experience. As a student, I had to learn a lot about the library and how to use it to create interactive data visualizations. One of the most difficult aspects for me was figuring out how to make the planets move and continuously move. It was a lot of work, but it was also really interesting to learn about these methods for how to create motion on websites. Another challenge was creating a user interface that was both informative and easy to use. I wanted to allow users to explore the star system at their own pace and learn about the different APOD planets. I also wanted to make sure that the visualization was visually appealing and engaging. I spent a lot of time experimenting with different sizes, colours, orbit speeds and fine details like the rotation on the axis of the APOD planets before I finally found something that I was happy with. Overall, I'm really glad that I had the opportunity to work on this project. It was a challenging but rewarding experience. I learned a lot about d3.js and data visualization, and I also gained a deeper understanding of data visualisation and web design.

How it felt

To better understand this creative journey here are some thoughts and feelings I experienced during the creation of this website:

  1. • Frustration: There were many times when I got stuck and didn't know how to proceed. I had to spend a lot of time on generative AI platforms, YouTube and online sources looking for answers (sometimes this was a dead-end).
  2. • Excitement: When I finally got something working, it was very exciting. I was especially excited when I got the orbits of the planets working correctly.
  3. • Challenge: I enjoyed the challenge of designing a complex and interactive visualization. It was a great opportunity to learn new skills and push myself outside of my comfort zone. I'm proud of the fact that I was able to overcome the challenges and create a successful project.

I would recommend this project to other students who are interested in learning about data visualization and d3.js and just being creative in terms of web design. It is a challenging but rewarding experience.

Data visualization, art, and representation

A commonly witnessed concept, data has become a large part of our daily lives. It is visible in nearly all aspects of human life from providing information useful information or serving as an aid in decision making or strategy creation. In modern times data has taken a new form due to how it is collected, interpreted, and depicted. Terms such as data visualization and data art have risen to give a better understanding of this concept of information understanding. This information presented through data visualization is powerful having the possibility of even swaying the opinions of those who interact with it due to the value and nature of representation strategies. Representation along with data visualization, data art and art in general all are intertwined in some shape or form and this once-complex connection that is slowly becoming more welcomed and understood by scholars, viewers and artists shall be discussed and analysed as to how deep this connection goes. Before delving into this relationship, it is important to break down and understand the parts of this connection.

Representation:

Representation is the binding force between data visualisation and art. This term can be understood as the “creation of meaning” (Kidd, 2016, p.19). This meaning-making is achieved through the use of signs and semiotics which are both grouped in a manner that builds codes and these codes help us construct meaning. Data and visualization:

Data can be understood as “raw, unprocessed information that has no meaning and can be organized in various forms” (Li, 2020, p.20). The forms are considered visualizations, and these visualizations are large in quantity, have their flaws and affordances and can be used in different ways to depict different ideas. The combination of the data and its being depicted is called data visualization. Data visualization can be understood as the depiction of information in a graphic manner which as a result gives the viewers of the information a way of understanding and critiquing information leading to the user being able to gain useful insights from the information such as being aware of patterns and how ideas correlate. Data visualisation can take on numerous forms each with its affordances and some of these forms are but not limited to trees, which have a hierarchical structure which allows for effective organization, maps, which are effective at highlighting relationships between elements and charts which are excellent for representing quantitative data. Data visualization is a very common tool of knowledge communication having prints in science such as air quality maps, molecular visualizations and weather report maps (Manorathna, 2023) and in businesses such as sale charts and stock graphs (Uber, n.d.) and in the arts.

Data art:

Art is a form of creative expression and as such data art is a way of using creative methods to relaying information regarding particular subjects. Data art has seen a rise in modern times and this is predominantly down to data, as per Roeser, “becoming a large part in cultural discourses and the lives of citizens and as such artist seek to join this movement by controlling the power of data” (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007). The rise in this type of knowledge communication is due to artists being afforded new software that allows for more artistic expression. Data art is crucial as it tends to enforce perspectives due to the way it is presented. This is due to how data art can exploit engagement and create new scenarios and perspectives. This can lead to data art being able to effectively deal with troubling societal issues most commonly such as technological risks posed but used to create the data visualizations. Regardless of the artist's intent of simply making art, artistic visualizations can often lead to biases in their visualisations. This is not the only drawback as data art visualizations tend to be hard to understand by the view as art itself is an abstract concept. This is a crucial issue as this goes against the main purpose of data visualizations. Not being able to comprehend what you are viewing is ineffective in efficient knowledge gain and as such the data visualisation may sometimes not serve its full purpose of providing knowledge. Data art also has a strict barrier to entry as some pursuits may not be considered art as a beautiful work does not automatically fall into the category of being artistic similarly not all visualisations are meant to be pretty and as such the aesthetic considerations of the data art are not a determining factor. Data art examples include Information is Beautiful (McCandless, n.d.) and Ground Resistance (Goatley, 2016).

Linking data visualisation, art and representation

It is important to understand how data art and data visualization are related and to this, an analysis of their representations shall be conducted. Data visualization is concerned with relaying information to enhance knowledge and understanding. Data art also focuses on this as well but takes a different approach by aiming to be thought-provoking, targeting the emotions of the viewer in order to form a deeper connection between the art and the viewer all in the hopes of conveying a narrative that does provide some form of knowledge. How they are presented also has a few contrasts to due most data visualizations being inanimate information that viewers can only usually interpret by seeing and reading. Data art also employs this tactic but takes it a step further through the use of interactivity. As mentioned before data art aims to draw in users and this interactivity is a way of establishing this connection as when the user is interacting with the data artwork, they feel more connected to it as a result of being able to “manipulate” it and better understand its forms. These two representations are not fully contradictory with a simple way of understanding them being data art being an extension and type of data visualization and data visualization being the umbrella term used to describe the various forms of knowledge communication. These two concepts also share a similar trait, which harms their nature, being that they can both be easily misinterpreted, are often hard to grasp, poorly constructed and executed and can illustrate false information. Both of these aspects do rely on basic principles of visualization such as clarity in depicted information, accuracy in terms of providing relevant and up-to-date information and also being visually comprehensible.

Why this information matters to an artist:

As a creative that is dabbling in the realm of artistic expression, knowing how to effectively use and communicate data art is essentially on a variety of scales. Knowing more about data ensures an expansion of one’s artistic capabilities. Data art presents a dynamic medium for artistic expression giving artists a new platform of being about to flex their creative muscles. As a creative, I can incorporate data into my creative process to experiment with new forms of visual storytelling, challenge artistic boundaries, and engage and discuss societal issues. Data art also holds the capability of enhancing artistic expression through data serving as a powerful tool for enhancing artistic expression. Data is pervasive in modern society, serving as a core reason for our understanding of the world and influencing our daily lives. By becoming skilled in data art, artists can address contemporary social, political, and environmental issues, triggering dialogues and raising awareness about data's impact on various walks of life. Expanding creative possibilities is an important consideration as data art offers a vast spectrum of creative possibilities allowing artists new ways to do things ensuring they evolve with the times. This ensures a connection with a wider audience is built due to being able to communicate ideas in an innovative and expansive language. The most important reason for being knowledgeable in the realm of data art is to correct preexisting drawbacks that exist in this form of knowledge communication as misrepresentation or lack of sufficient information is a common theme that is a result of not creating good data art visualizations.

Concluding:

Data art emerges as an intriguing medium for artistic expression, blending the complexities of data and an artist's creative vision. This merger empowers artists to challenge traditional artistic boundaries, deal with new forms of visual storytelling, and address contemporary societal issues. As data's intricacies in our lives continue to grow in complexity, data art aims to serve an increasingly prominent role in the art world. Artists who embrace this medium position themselves at the forefront of this evolving and growing movement by expanding their creative zones, reaching with a wider audience, and contributing to the ever-changing landscape of art in the modern world.
